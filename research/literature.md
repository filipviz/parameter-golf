I'm competing in OpenAI's [Parameter Golf competition](https://github.com/openai/parameter-golf/). It's in the spirit of [modded-nanogpt](https://github.com/KellerJordan/modded-nanogpt), but with different rules:
- Code and compressed model are under 16 MB (i.e. 16e6 bytes).
- Trains in under 10 minutes on 8xH100 SXM.
- Evaluated by compression (cross-entropy) on the FineWeb validation set in bits per byte.
- Evaluation is allowed at any sequence length.
- They reserve the right to disqualify runs that are not in the spirit of challenge (i.e. sneaking in compute unfairly).
- Runs must show at `p < 0.01` that they achieved the required 0.005-nat improvement (due to inter-run variance).

Basic considerations:
- Most of the records so far are based on small one-off tweaks, and aren't motivated by unified theoretical premises, rigorous mathematics, or empirically derived scaling laws. We can win by being more rigorous.
- Records so far have an MFU of ~16-19%. We may be able to improve occupancy with larger, denser matrices. If we can find recipes which compress these really well that could be a promising path forwards.
- These models are 15-20x overtrained relative to Chinchilla-optimal. The 16 MB limit is clearly the bottleneck on performance.
- This competition measures BPB, not accuracy. Techniques which are loss-neutral but improve downstream accuracy are usually great, but don't matter for us.

[The baseline](https://github.com/openai/parameter-golf/blob/main/train_gpt.py) has quite an idiosyncratic shape, which has been adopted by later runs. It's quite shallow (9 layers), and has atypical choices like a 2x MLP dimension multiplier (rather than something closer to 4x). Other runs have inherited these choices. My theory is that by doing more rigorous experimentation, we can find a better approach to basic structural hyperparameters like depth vs. width, d_vocab, batch size, and so on. Then we can individually validate and stack later wins on top of this.

Key resources:
- Other submissions and records for this repo. That said, we should take these with a grain of salt. I get the sense that most people are blindly allowing AI agents to try out hundreds of minor tweaks/variants and selecting what nudges validation BPB down. These techniques are often overfit, or work for opaque reasons, and may not generalize to our approach.
- [modded-nanogpt](https://github.com/KellerJordan/modded-nanogpt): the challenge there is to reach `<= 3.28` FineWeb validation loss on `8xH100` as fast as possible, with a fixed number of active parameters (something like ~236M). This *active* parameter constraint is part of why embedding-heavy techniques show up so often there. These may transfer poorly to Parameter Golf. Otherwise, this is one of the most highly-tuned baselines on a smaller transformer, so is a great source of inspiration.
- [nanochat](https://github.com/karpathy/nanochat) is Andrej Karpathy's `modded-nanogpt` variant which reproduces the full modern LLM pipeline (including SFT and RL). This repository is a great source of information for a number of reasons:
    - Karpathy is a great researcher and doesn't blindly accept results.
    - He validates improvements across scales, as described in his `dev/LOG.md`. Although he validates at *larger* scales than `modded-nanogpt`, this means that he's likely to find ideas which are more generalizable.
    - The repository is still performance-oriented.
    - Karpathy provides detailed writeups on the theoretical motivations behind his approaches on GitHub discussions. The [miniseries](https://github.com/karpathy/nanochat/discussions/420) writeup is a particularly useful look at his approach to scaling laws, and [this writeup](https://github.com/karpathy/nanochat/discussions/481) is a great look at which popular techiques did and didn't work.
- [slowrun](https://github.com/qlabs-eng/slowrun): `modded-nanogpt` adapted to the finite-data regime. This is less competitive so we can't trust it as much as the aforementioned repositories, but it still may be a good source of training efficiency ideas. That said, we're *overtraining* and have lots of data relative to our model size, so this won't be as applicable.

Research literature:
1. [Scaling Laws with Vocabulary: Larger Models Deserve Larger Vocabularies](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.13623) studies vocabulary size scaling within BPE. Its key finding: optimal vocab scales as N_v ∝ N_nv^0.83. Critically for us, it shows that overtrained models benefit from larger vocabularies (and our model is 15-20x overtrained). However, the paper doesn't study models as small as ours, doesn't compare BPE vs other algorithms, and doesn't account for a compressed-size budget constraint (our 16MB limit). The Parameter Golf baseline uses `d_model=512` and `d_vocab=1024`, meaning our embedding matrix (which is tied) is a tiny fraction of our total parameters - my takeaway from this paper is that we should experiment with larger vocab sizes (2048 and 4096)! It'd also be quite interesting to try one run at a vocab of 512.
2. The broader small-model literature is pretty consistent: deep, thin, tied, GQA, overtrained. [Qwen3-0.6B](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09388) is 28 layers at hidden size 1024 with 16/8 GQA, tied embeddings, and about 152k vocab; [SmolLM2-360M](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.02737) is 32 layers at 960 with 15/5 GQA, tied embeddings, 49,152 vocab, and was trained on 4T tokens; [Baguettotron](https://huggingface.co/PleIAs/Baguettotron) is an extreme 80-layer, 576-d, 9/3 GQA model with tied embeddings and 65,536 vocab, trained on 200B tokens. [MobileLLM](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.14905)'s ablations are even more explicit: for sub-billion models, it finds shallow models weak and puts the best depth near 30 layers. However, these are all in the ~hundreds of millions of parameters range, meaning the results may be different at our scale.
3. [MobileLLM](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.14905) is highly relevant. Their depth vs. width ablations at a fixed 135M parameter budget found that a 30-layer model performed best.
4. [MiniCPM](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.06395) found that a warmup-stable-decay LR schedule is particularly useful for small models, and that decaying for 10% of training is sufficient to achieve optimal loss. They used exponential annealing `lr = peak * 0.5^((s-S)/T)`. Interestingly, their 1.2B parameter model was *deeper* than their 2.4B parameter model. Their MLP multiplier was 2.5x. They had a very high peak LR of 0.01.
